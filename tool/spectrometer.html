The problem

One number hides a population that disagrees

  • A single brand-health score averages a press corps that adores you, owners who are lukewarm, and a forum that's openly hostile — and points your spend at an "average customer" who exists nowhere.
  • The variance that wrecks a flat score is exactly the signal you need. This instrument measures the variance — not around it.

The instrument's scale

Eight dimensions of a perception

A brand is one signal made of these eight. Two audiences can hold the same overall opinion while reading those dimensions completely differently — the same score hiding two different brands. Toggle the audiences below to see it.

Each dimension carries two readings. One is how strongly it registers — the score, 0–10 (the line's height). The other is which way it leans — its valence:

−1 — hostile / negative
0 — neutral / mixed
+1 — favourable / positive

Strength is not approval: an audience can read a brand as intensely economic and feel negative about it. On the spectrum strip the dot slides from negative (left) to positive (right) along each line — a line sitting high with its dot on the left is the dangerous one: an audience that cares a lot, and not in your favour.

A real read

Five cohorts, one brand

A spectrum strip: eight wavelength lanes, one per dimension. Each audience is a line at its score (height = how strongly the dimension registers), the dot on the line is its valence (left −1 negative … right +1 positive), and the band is the spread across audiences. Valence is floored: where the lean sits below the instrument's noise floor the dot turns hollow and grey at centre — the read is sub-resolution, so the tool abstains rather than fake a direction. No radar — a spider chart's silhouette is an artifact of axis order, not the brand. Turn the detail dial up to add sentiment, uncertainty and the noise floor; each step says what it still hides. The managerial reading

What you actually do with it

  • Pick the cohort that's moving — not the average.
  • Pick the dimension that carries it — target the wavelength, not "the brand."
  • Act only above the floor — spend against real separations; ignore sub-resolution noise.
  • Track drift over time — re-read the same cohorts across a campaign and watch what moved.
