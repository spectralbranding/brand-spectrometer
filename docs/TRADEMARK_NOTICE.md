## 2. Fair-Use Rationale

The Brand Spectrometer relies on standard nominative, descriptive, and referential fair-use doctrines under both United States and European Union trademark law:

- **United States — Nominative Fair Use.** Brand marks are used to refer to the brands themselves where no reasonable substitute identifier exists. Use is limited to what is necessary, and nothing in this work suggests sponsorship or endorsement by the mark owner. See *New Kids on the Block v. News America Publishing*, 971 F.2d 302 (9th Cir. 1992); *Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A. v. Tabari*, 610 F.3d 1171 (9th Cir. 2010).
- **United States — Statutory Descriptive Fair Use.** To the extent any brand term is used in its ordinary descriptive sense, that use is protected under Lanham Act § 33(b)(4), 15 U.S.C. § 1115(b)(4). See *KP Permanent Make-Up, Inc. v. Lasting Impression I, Inc.*, 543 U.S. 111 (2004).
- **United States — First Amendment Commentary and Research.** Use of brand marks in analytical, critical, and research commentary on publicly observable brand artifacts is protected expression. See *Rogers v. Grimaldi*, 875 F.2d 994 (2d Cir. 1989); *Mattel, Inc. v. Walking Mountain Productions*, 353 F.3d 792 (9th Cir. 2003). The author has reviewed *Jack Daniel's Properties, Inc. v. VIP Products LLC*, 599 U.S. 140 (2023), which narrowed the *Rogers* test where a junior user employs another's mark as a source identifier for its own goods; this work does **not** use any third-party mark as a source identifier for the Brand Spectrometer itself or for any product the author markets.
- **European Union — Article 14 EUTMR.** Use is limited to descriptive and referential identification of the goods or services of the trade-mark proprietor, in accordance with honest practices in industrial and commercial matters. See Regulation (EU) 2017/1001, Art. 14; *Gillette Co. v. LA-Laboratories Ltd Oy*, Case C-228/03 (ECJ, 17 Mar. 2005).
- **Italy — Codice della Proprietà Industriale.** Use is consistent with the limitations set out in Article 21 of Legislative Decree No. 30 of 10 February 2005, as amended.

## 3. What This Work Does Not Do

The Brand Spectrometer, in all public outputs published under this repository:

- Does **not** reproduce any logo, wordmark, monogram, trade dress, livery color, font, or other graphic brand asset of any third party.
- Does **not** include any image, icon, or favicon derived from a third-party brand asset.
- Does **not** use any third-party brand name in repository names, package names, domain names, social-media handles, or product names of the Brand Spectrometer itself.
- Does **not** combine any brand mark with terms such as "official," "authorized," "approved," "certified," or "partner."
- Does **not** create or distribute derivative works of any brand asset.
- Does **not** sell, license, or monetize any product or service labeled with, identified by, or evoking the source-identifying function of any third-party brand mark.
- Does **not** claim to disclose, possess, or infer the actual internal brand specifications, brand-management documents, or confidential strategy of any brand owner.

## 4. Reader's Interpretive Notice

The analytical outputs of the Brand Spectrometer — including dimensional scores, cohort-indexed spec inferences, perception-cloud reconstructions, and cross-cohort variance reports — are **research conclusions about publicly observable artifacts**, produced by an external observer operating on public information. They are not statements of fact about any brand owner's actual internal specifications, plans, or intentions.

Under Spectral Brand Theory, brand specifications inferred from public artifacts are cohort-dependent observer-side reconstructions. Multiple distinct internal specifications can produce identical observable signals (metamerism). The methodology does not assume a single ground truth exists, and readers should not interpret outputs as definitive accounts of any brand owner's position.
