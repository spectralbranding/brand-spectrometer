# Schema Design Notes — Brand Spectrometer v0.1

This schema does not assume a platonic brand essence exists. It records cohort-indexed observer-side reconstructions; metameric variance across cohorts is the primary measurement, not noise around a true value. There is no ground truth against which a single "the brand spec" could be validated, and the schema is built to make that absence operational rather than to hide it. An atlas that would collapse to a single point estimate is structurally disallowed by validation rule 2 (minimum five cohorts) and structurally penalised by variance reporting (cross-cohort sigma plus a composite metameric_degree are required fields, not optional commentary).

## 1. Schema design overview

`atlas_schema_v0.1.yaml` defines a three-block document: atlas-level metadata, a `cohorts` map keyed by cohort_id, and a top-level `variance` block plus `provenance` block. Each cohort carries its own inferred_spec across the eight SBT dimensions in canonical order (semiotic / narrative / ideological / experiential / social / economic / cultural / temporal), confidence intervals, observable Tier-3 signals, observable Tier-5 process couplings, and a per-cohort renderer / extractor operator pair. The variance block reports per-dimension cross-cohort sigma, a composite metameric_degree, and the temporal drift window the variance figures characterise. The provenance block contains a flat `artifact_inventory` with retrieval metadata and SHA-256 per artifact, plus relative-path pointers to the JSONL LLM-call manifest and the prompt purity protocol document. `validate_atlas.py` enforces eleven structural rules listed at the end of the schema YAML and printed verbatim in the script docstring.

Three discipline choices warrant flagging. First, the schema enforces multi-cohort sampling at the validator: a minimum of five cohorts plus at least three artifacts per cohort. A single-cohort atlas is rejected. Second, cross-operator discipline is enforced per cohort: the renderer operator and the extractor operator must differ within each cohort, replicating the 2026ap Paper B pattern at the cohort level rather than only at the atlas level. Third, the eight SBT dimensions must appear in the canonical order — order is structurally enforced, not just presence — to prevent silent re-ordering across atlases.

## 2. Mapping to corpus anchors

**2026v — R15 Dimensional Collapse in AI-Mediated Brand Perception: Large Language Models as Metameric Observers (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.19422427).** The R15 thesis is that distinct underlying brand structures can produce indistinguishable observable signal patterns under bounded observer capacity, and that the cross-observer variance is the measurement. The atlas operationalises this directly: variance.cross_cohort_sigma and variance.metameric_degree are required outputs; a single-cohort atlas (which would deny the variance) is structurally disallowed.

**2026ap — Same Meaning, Different Prose: Spine Preservation and Rendering Equivalence in Organizational Knowledge Work (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20409701).** Paper B establishes that renderer-extractor operator separation eliminates within-model memory contamination as a confound on rendering-equivalence evidence. The schema enforces this at the cohort level: every cohort declares a renderer_operator_id and an extractor_operator_id, and the validator rejects any cohort where the two are equal. The Ferrari skeleton further rotates pairings across cohorts to bound family co-bias and routes Mandarin artifacts to a Chinese-native operator pair.

**2026ah — Brand as a Modular Layer: Tiered Organizational Architecture, Separability, and Firm Performance in Multi-Brand Strategies (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.19930157).** The atlas places brand at Tier 4 (the product-specification surface) consistent with 2026ah. Tier 3 (Business Entity) and Tier 5 (Process) signals are captured as separate per-cohort fields (`inferred_tier_3_signals`, `inferred_tier_5_process_couplings`) precisely because they are NOT brand; the atlas records what each cohort can see of the adjacent tiers without conflating those signals into the brand spec.

**2026ag — Dual Hierarchies of Organizational Transferability: A Six-Tier Ontology and Theory of Acquisition Failure Propagation (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.19895813).** The six-tier ontology is the structural backbone: the atlas treats Tier 4 as the primary inference target while preserving Tier-3 and Tier-5 observability as independent records. The schema does not refer to Tier 1 (Owner Intent), Tier 2 (Business Model), or Tier 6 (Organization) because those tiers are rarely observable from public artifact subsets at cohort grain; future schema versions may extend.

**2026ao — Spec-Based Research in the Post-AI Era: A Cost-Asymmetry Theory of Meaning and Meaningfulness in Organizational Knowledge Work (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20409683).** Paper A introduces the operator role as level-of-analysis above human-vs-AI instance. The atlas reflects this by typing renderer_operator_id and extractor_operator_id as model-version strings rather than human-vs-AI flags: any operator-class instance (Claude, GPT-4o, Gemini, Qwen, DeepSeek, or future-model) is treated identically at the schema layer, and the operator-pair declaration is the unit of provenance.

## 4. Metameric-degree formula choice and rationale

The schema specifies `metameric_degree = 1 - mean_pairwise_cosine_similarity` across the C(N, 2) unordered pairs of cohort eight-dimensional spec vectors. The alternatives considered were mean pairwise Euclidean distance (normalised by the maximum possible Euclidean distance in [0, 10]^8) and the determinant of the cohort-vector covariance matrix.

Cosine dissimilarity was chosen for three reasons. First, it is scale-invariant on the dimensional pattern: a cohort that scores all eight dimensions uniformly low has the same shape as a cohort that scores all eight dimensions uniformly high, and cosine treats them as identical. This is the right behaviour for brand-spec inference because cohorts with sparse artifact coverage tend to produce uniformly damped scores rather than zeroed dimensions — penalising them on magnitude would conflate artifact-coverage variance with spec-interpretation variance. Second, cosine on non-negative vectors in [0, 10]^8 is bounded in [0, 1], so `1 - cosine` is also bounded in [0, 1] and admits a stable categorical interpretation (e.g., metameric_degree < 0.1 = well-defined spec; > 0.3 = high cohort divergence). Euclidean would need an arbitrary normalisation constant. Third, cosine is well understood in the corpus already — R0, R1, R2, R17, R21 all treat cosine on observer-cohort vectors as a primary similarity measure — and importing it here keeps the cross-paper vocabulary consistent.

The cost of cosine is that it ignores absolute magnitude entirely. Two cohorts that agree on dimensional emphasis but disagree on overall intensity (one cohort rates Ferrari at uniformly 9s, another at uniformly 5s) will have cosine similarity 1.0 and metameric_degree 0. Whether this is a feature or a defect is a judgement call. For Brand Spectrometer atlases the dimensional pattern is what we want to measure; absolute intensity is more a function of cohort artifact density and rhetorical register than of spec interpretation. The atlas already reports per-dimension cross-cohort sigma, which captures magnitude variance separately. The composite metameric_degree captures shape divergence; sigma captures magnitude divergence; the two together describe the cohort spread.

Open: whether to publish a second composite (`magnitude_divergence`) computed as the standard deviation of cohort-vector L2 norms. Deferred to v0.2 pending Phase 1.3 worked-example experience.

## 5. Open questions for orchestrator review

1. **Single composite vs two composites.** Should v0.1 ship with only `metameric_degree` (cosine-based shape divergence), or should it also ship a `magnitude_divergence` (L2-norm spread) composite? Current schema ships only metameric_degree; the per-dimension cross_cohort_sigma block carries the magnitude information in finer grain. Decision before Phase 1.3 would let the Phase 1.3 sub-agents populate the second figure.

2. **Tier-3 / Tier-5 signal vocabulary.** The schema currently treats `inferred_tier_3_signals` and `inferred_tier_5_process_couplings` as free-text string lists. A controlled vocabulary (e.g., trademark visibility / IP disclosure / corporate-entity mention for Tier 3; manufacturing location / supplier mention / fulfilment cadence for Tier 5) would tighten cross-atlas comparability but constrain phenomena the schema may not anticipate. Recommend leaving as free-text for v0.1 and revisiting after Phase 1.3 surfaces what cohorts actually report.

3. **Operator-pair declaration grain.** The schema declares renderer / extractor at the cohort level, on the assumption that each cohort runs one rendering and one extraction. If a cohort is processed across multiple artifact batches with different operator pairs (e.g., Mandarin artifacts via Qwen / DeepSeek and English-language artifacts within the same cohort via Claude / GPT-4o), the current schema would need a list-of-pairs extension. Recommend deferring to v0.2 unless Phase 1.3 chinese-cohort artifact mix forces it sooner.

4. **Skeleton validity.** The Ferrari skeleton intentionally fails the validator on the artifact-count rule (0 < 3 per cohort). This is desired behaviour: the skeleton is not yet a complete atlas, and the validator surface the gap. Phase 1.3 sub-agents will populate artifacts and re-run validate_atlas.py to confirm pass. If a "skeleton mode" is wanted that suppresses the artifact-count error, recommend adding a top-level `status: skeleton` flag in v0.2 rather than weakening the validator.

5. **Cross-operator manifest format.** The schema points to a JSONL manifest at `llm_call_manifest_path` but does not lock the manifest schema. The intent is to reuse `research/code/llm_call_logger.py` per PUBLIC_MIRROR_STANDARD. Phase 1.3 should confirm the logger's per-call JSONL line schema covers operator_role (renderer vs extractor vs orchestrator) explicitly; if not, a small extension is required before Phase 1.3 begins.

## 6. Atlas composition pattern (multi-unit atlases) — STANDING DECISION

**Decision (2026-05-29, user-approved during Pepsi vs Olipop Phase 1.5.0): a single-brand, cohort-indexed spec is the atomic, schema-valid unit. Any *relation* between units — a brand-vs-brand gap, a brand-over-time drift — is expressed as a separate overlay layer, never by mutating the atomic unit's schema.**

This is the default for all future atlases. It is not an ad-hoc choice for the two-brand case: it is the consistent generalization of the stance METHODOLOGY §6 already takes on the temporal axis (drift is computed *across* snapshots and explicitly must not be smoothed into a single atlas). The composition pattern applies that same architectural decision to the brand axis.

### Decision rule

| Situation | Structure |
|---|---|
| 1 brand, 1 window | 1 `atlas.yaml` (e.g., Ferrari Luce) — no overlay |
| M brands compared | M `atlas.yaml` (each a valid single-brand atlas) + 1 `comparison.yaml` overlay |
| 1 brand, K windows | K snapshot `atlas.yaml` + 1 drift overlay (per METHODOLOGY §6) |
| M brands × K windows | M×K `atlas.yaml` + comparison + drift overlays |

### Why this and not the alternatives

- **vs a top-level `brands:` map in one file**: the map forks `validate_atlas.py` and re-narrates how single-brand atlases relate to the schema; the per-brand variance block has to be duplicated inside the map anyway, so the map buys structural cost without removing the duplication it was meant to avoid.
- **vs dual/N sub-vectors per cohort**: breaks validator rule 5 (one 8-dim `inferred_spec` per cohort) and the single-variance-block schema; requires per-key special-casing across the validator and aggregator; collapses entirely at N > 2.
- **Composition wins on**: zero validator/schema fork (each unit validates under the current `schema_version` unchanged); each unit keeps an independent `metameric_degree` (so a per-brand metameric asymmetry — e.g., one brand fragmented/high-variance, another coherent/low-variance — falls out directly and stays comparable to single-brand atlases); `aggregate.py` is the existing single-unit aggregation run M times plus a thin overlay step; scales from pairwise to N-way (the overlay generalises from one gap to an N×N per-cohort gap matrix).

### Accepted cost

Cohort *metadata* (label, size estimate, operator pairing) is duplicated across the per-unit files. This is accepted in favour of self-containment: every unit file is independently valid and reproducible. Revisit only if a future atlas has enough units that the duplication becomes a maintenance burden; even then, prefer a generator that emits self-contained files over a shared definition file that breaks per-file validity.

### Overlay file convention

`comparison.yaml` (brand axis) carries: `brands` list, `cohorts_shared` list, `per_cohort_gap` (1 − cosine between the unit vectors per cohort + per-dimension delta), `cross_cohort_gap_agreement` (mean/sigma of the gap across cohorts + any reversal cohorts), and any external-benchmark comparison block. Drift overlays follow the analogous shape on the time axis per METHODOLOGY §6. Overlays are not validated by `validate_atlas.py` (which validates atomic units only); a dedicated `validate_comparison.py` is a v0.2 tooling item.
